Abdi-Heba

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Abdi-Heba (Abdi-Kheba, Abdi-Hepat, or Abdi-Hebat) was king of Jerusalem during the Amarna period (mid 1300s BC). Abdi-Heba's name can be translated as "servant of Hebat", a Hurrian goddess. Some scholars believe the correct reading is Ebed-Nob. Whether Abdi-Heba was himself of Hurrian descent is unknown, as is the relationship between the general populace of pre-Israelite Jerusalem (known as Jebusites in the Bible) and the Hurrians.

One of the Amarna letters, (note paragraph divisions).

Also unknown is whether he was part of a dynasty that governed Jerusalem or whether he was put on the throne by the Egyptians. Abdi-Heba himself notes that he holds his kingdom not through his parents but by the grace of Pharaoh, but this might be flattery rather than an accurate representation of the situation.

[edit] Correspondence with Egypt

During Abdi-Heba's reign the region was under attack from marauding bands of Habiru.[1] Abdi-Heba made frequent pleas to the Pharaoh of Egypt (probably Amenhotep III for an army[2] or, at least, an officer to command[3], as well as for other military aid in fighting off his enemies, both Canaanite warlords and bands of Habiru.

Say to the king, my lord: Message of Abdi-Heba, your servent. I fall at the feet of my lord seven times and seven times. Consider the entire affair. Milkilu and Tagi brought troops into Qiltu against me... May the king provide for his land. Consider the lands of Gazru Gezer, Ashqaluna (Ashkelon) and Lakisi (Lachish). They have given [my enemies] food, oil and any other requirement. So may the king provide for archers and send the archers against men that commit crimes against the king, my lord. If this year there are archers, then the lands and the hazzanu (client kings) will belong to the king, my lord. But if there are no archers, then the king will have neither lands nor hazzanu. Consider Jerusalem! This neither my father nor my mother gave to me. The strong hand of the king gave it to me. Consider the deed! This is the deed of Milkilu and the deed of the sons of Labaya, who have given the land of the king to the 'Apiru (Habiru).[4]

As a result, conspiracy charges are made against Abdi Heba, who defended himself strenuously in his correspondence with Pharaoh.[5]

In later years Abdi-Heba appears to have reconciled with the Habiru, or at least certain bands of them, and hired mercenaries from among their ranks. Indeed, though he earlier complained about the depredations of Labaya, Shuwardata, king of the Canaanite town of Keilah as well as other places in the Judean highlands, refers to him as a "new Labaya":

Say to the king, my lord, my god, my Sun: Message of Shuwardata, your servant, the dirt at your feet. I fall at the feet of the king, my lord, my god, my Sun, seven times and seven times. The king, my lord, permitted me to wage war against Qiltu (Keilah). I waged war. It is now at peace with me; my city is restored to me. Why did Abdi-Heba write to the men of Qiltu, "Accept silver and follow me?"... Moreover, Labaya, who used to take our towns, is dead, but now another Labaya is Abdi-Heba, and he seizes our town...[6]

Abdi-Heba's ultimate fate is unknown.

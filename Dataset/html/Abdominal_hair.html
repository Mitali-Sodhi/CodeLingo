Abdominal hair

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The term abdominal hair refers to the hair that grows on the abdomen (belly) of humans and non-human mammals, in the region between the pubic area and the thorax (chest). The growth of abdominal hair follows the same pattern on nearly all mammals, vertically from the pubic area upwards and from the thorax downwards to the umbilicus (navel). The abdominal hair of non-human mammals is part of the pelage or fur.

[edit] Human abdominal hair

Image:Abdominal hair.jpg
Hair on Male Abdomen displaying the Acuminate pattern of abdominal hair growth.

Before puberty, the adominal region of both males and females is covered with very fine vellus hair. In response to rising levels of androgens (mainly testosterone) during and after puberty, the skin of the abdomen begins to produce terminal hair with a faster growth rate. This process affects primarily men and is developing during puberty corresponding to Tanner stage 5. Initially hair grows in vertical lines from the pubic area up and from the thorax down to the navel. Slang terms for these lines include “happy trail”, “funny trail”, “crab ladder”, “joy trail”, “snail trail” or “treasure trail”. With growing age the hair may spread to the remainder of the abdomen. Although the development of abdominal hair normally begins during puberty it may also start later, between the age of 20 and 30. The growth continues subsequently. It is now quite common for many men, especially young men, to remove their abdominal hair, by shaving, waxing, or other means.

Some women may develop a small line of hair from the pubic area up to the navel. According to the female sex image in many cultures this is often seen as undesirable and unattractive. An excessive abdominal hair on women following the male pattern can be referred to hirsutism.

[edit] Patterns of Abdominal Hair

Various studies of European subjects have documented four general patterns of pubic and abdominal hair including:

  • Horizontal - Characterized by upper surface of pubic hair terminating in a horizontal line with no further extension hair to the abdomen.
  • Sagittal - Resembles the first but with the addition of a narrow vertical band of hair extending from the pubic hair towards the navel.
  • Acuminate - Pattern considered a classical masculine shape. Characterized by an inverted 'V' pattern extending upward from the pubic hair. Upper limit may end below the navel, at the navel, above the navel or near the chest. (Example shown in accompaning photograph.)[1]
  • Disperse (or quadrangular) - Hair is distributed broadly over the abdomen without forming a discrete geometric pattern.[2]

Pattern one is most common in females with an incidence of about 80 percent. This pattern is also common in males younger than 25 with an incidence of about 40 percent in that age category. As males continue to mature many develop the acuminate or disperse pattern. Pattern two occurs in about 17 percent of females and 6 percent of males. Pattern three occurs in about 55 percent of males and occasionally in females. Pattern 4 occurs in about 19 percent of the males studied. [3]
